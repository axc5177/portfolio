01
Background & Problem Space

The discovery paradox

Spotify has 600M+ users. Yet research shows that most heavy users default to the same 20–40 songs on repeat — despite having access to 100M+ tracks. Why does "more" lead to "less" discovery behavior?

Business Context
Why This Matters to Spotify
Discovery drives playlist saves, premium upgrades, and long-term retention. Users who actively discover music have 2.3× higher 12-month retention than passive listeners.
User Problem
The Friction of Exploration
Despite rich recommendation data, users report feeling overwhelmed, not inspired by discovery surfaces. The experience is optimized for comfort, not curiosity.
Research Question
What We're Investigating
What specific behavioral patterns and UI friction points cause musically curious users to disengage from discovery and retreat to familiar content?
02
Research Methodology

How the study was conducted

Mixed-methods approach: qualitative interviews + behavioral self-reporting diary over 2 weeks, focused on daily music-finding behavior in natural contexts.

01
Participant Recruitment & Screener
Recruited 5 daily Spotify users aged 19–28 via personal network. Screened for: 30+ min/day usage, mix of premium/free, varied musical tastes. Purposefully avoided power users to capture mainstream behavior patterns.
02
Semi-Structured Interviews (45 min each)
In-person and video sessions. Protocol covered: typical music sessions, last time they discovered something new, moments of frustration, workarounds they've invented. Sessions recorded and transcribed with consent.
03
2-Week Behavioral Diary
Participants logged 3 Spotify sessions per day using a simple template: What played → How they chose it → Satisfaction (1–5) → Would they listen again? Generated 210 individual session data points.
04
Affinity Mapping & Thematic Analysis
Synthesized interview transcripts using affinity mapping. Grouped 87 individual observations into behavioral themes. Cross-validated themes across diary data to confirm frequency and consistency.

05
Key Findings

Three patterns explain everything

9.1
Algorithm Tunnel Vision — The Feedback Loop Trap
All 5 participants reported that Spotify's recommendations feel increasingly narrow over time. The algorithm optimizes for skip prevention (engagement), not exploration. Users' "risky" listening — the moments that could spark discovery — is penalized by an algorithm trying to minimize skips. The result: a comfort prison disguised as personalization.
Mentioned by: 5/5 participants · 34 diary entries · SEVERITY 9.1/10
7.8
Context Collapse — The Wrong Recommendation at the Right Moment
Users' moods and contexts shift dramatically through the day (gym → commute → study → social), but Spotify serves one unified taste profile. 4/5 participants described deliberately ignoring recommendations because "it's not that kind of moment." The app doesn't know if you're working, running, or winding down.
Mentioned by: 4/5 participants · 28 diary entries · SEVERITY 7.8/10
6.4
Discovery Anxiety — The Paralysis of Too Many Choices
When users do venture to explore, surfaces like Browse or Search overwhelm them with options but provide insufficient scaffolding. The cognitive load of "what do I want to find?" without low-stakes guidance causes users to retreat to the familiar — especially under time pressure.
Mentioned by: 3/5 participants · 19 diary entries · SEVERITY 6.4/10

08
Design Recommendations

Four things Spotify could build next

1
🧭
Context-Aware Listening Modes
Let users tag their current context before a session (Working / Running / Exploring / Winding Down). Surface recommendations optimized for that moment, keeping context-specific behavior separate from the core taste profile.
↑ Discovery frequency
est. +28% exploration sessions
2
🔬
Exploration Sandbox Mode
An opt-in "exploration mode" that explicitly signals to the algorithm: "I'm trying new things, don't penalize skips." After the session, users choose what to integrate into their taste profile — giving them agency over the algorithm.
↑ Algorithm trust
addresses top user complaint
3
💬
Transparent "Why This?" Explanations
Surface a brief, plain-English reason for each recommendation ("Because you loved X · Similar guitar tone"). This builds trust, reduces skip anxiety, and teaches users how to navigate the algorithm rather than fear it.
↑ Recommendation engagement
reduces skip rates on new music
4
👥
Trusted Social Discovery Layer
Show what music specific friends (with mutual sharing enabled) have been discovering this week — not as a social feed, but as a quiet, high-trust recommendation layer. Friends are more trusted than algorithms for music recommendations (4.2 vs 2.8 avg trust rating).
↑ Social feature adoption
aligns with trust data from study
09
Reflection & Limitations

What I'd do differently

Study Limitations
Small Sample, Homogeneous Group
5 participants, all college-aged, from personal network. Findings should be validated with a larger, more diverse group — particularly older users and non-students who may have different discovery patterns. Convenience sampling introduces self-selection bias.
Next Steps
If This Were a Real Project
I would validate with a 50-participant survey instrument, run usability tests on wireframed prototypes of the Context Mode, and conduct a 30-day longitudinal diary study. A/B testing the "exploration sandbox" at 5% of users would be the ideal product validation path.
